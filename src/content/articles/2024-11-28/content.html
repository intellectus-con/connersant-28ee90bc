Financial markets are often described as mechanisms for price discovery—vast computational systems that aggregate information about economic value. But this description, while accurate, misses something essential. Markets are also meaning-making machines.

Every price is a story. Every trade is an argument. The numbers on the screen represent not just economic calculations but collective beliefs about the future, about risk, about what matters.

The Narrative Layer

Economists have long struggled with the disconnect between market behavior and rational models. Prices move on rumors, on sentiment, on narratives that have little connection to fundamental value.

{{image:0}}

But perhaps this isn't a bug—it's a feature. Markets don't just process information; they create shared understanding. They give us a common language to talk about uncertainty, a framework for coordinating expectations across millions of actors who will never meet.

Implications for Investors

Understanding markets as meaning-makers changes how we think about investment. Technical analysis isn't just pattern recognition; it's reading collective psychology. Value investing isn't just finding discrepancies; it's betting on convergence between story and reality.

The most successful investors aren't those who ignore narratives, but those who understand them while maintaining independence from them.
