We live in an attention economy, but we build environments for a different species. Our offices, our devices, our cities—all designed as if human attention were infinite, unflagging, mechanically reliable.

The mismatch between our cognitive architecture and our built environment creates friction we've learned to ignore. We adapt to interruption, normalize distraction, mistake busyness for productivity.

Designing for Focus

What would our environments look like if designed around attention's true nature? The answer involves both addition and subtraction.

Addition: cues that direct focus, transitions that signal context switches, spaces that support deep work.

Subtraction: the elimination of unnecessary choices, the reduction of ambient interruption, the removal of friction from meaningful tasks.

The Personal Architecture

Beyond physical space, we each construct personal architectures of attention through habit, routine, and tool selection. These architectures can be intentional or accidental, supportive or sabotaging.

The most productive people are often those who have thought carefully about their attention architecture—who treat focus not as willpower but as design.
